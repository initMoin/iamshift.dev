I am Moinuddin Ahmad.

I build and design as one process. Everything else, skating, balling, coding, and creating, is grounded in responsibility, family, and faith.

current state

I am currently rebuilding ToDo with the intention of getting the foundation right. This is not an update or a redesign, but a reset, and the focus is on defining a structure that will hold over time instead of requiring constant correction later. This site is going through the same process, where anything that does not belong is removed and what remains is built with intention rather than decoration.

I work best when I am slightly outside my comfort zone because it forces better decisions and removes the option to rely on habit. Most of the process starts with the experience, where I define how something should feel and then build into that, adjusting until it holds. Design and development are not separate steps here, and when something feels off, it is corrected at the source instead of being layered over.

There is a clear standard that everything is held against, and anything that does not align with it does not stay. The goal is not to preserve effort, but to preserve direction, which means being willing to remove work even when it functions as intended.

I built a feature that worked exactly as expected. It pulled attention away from what mattered and disrupted the direction of the product. It was removed. Time spent does not justify keeping something that weakens the system, and if a component drifts from the core, it gets cut.

ongoing loops

My mornings are consistent, and that consistency carries into the rest of the day. Fajr comes first, followed by some time after sunrise, then breakfast and coffee. I sit down with my bullet journal to clear my head before starting work. It helps establish clarity before anything else begins.

When I begin, I do not start cold. I make tea, say Bismillah and my duas, and review notes from the previous session before touching anything. That context matters, and it keeps me from wasting time figuring out where I left off.

Lately, I have been paying close attention to where software engineering is heading. The landscape is shifting quickly with the introduction of AI, and I am working through it with a focus on understanding both its weaknesses and its strengths. At the same time, it presents an opportunity to reinforce the fundamentals, focusing on structure, cleaner code, and making more informed decisions with data structures and algorithms.

Alongside that, feature creep remains a constant pressure. It does not need much to take hold, which means it has to be managed with intent and kept within clear boundaries.

personal signals

I am currently reading Project Hail Mary. My family recommended it, and my son wants to watch the film, so I am finishing the book first. I do not watch adaptations before reading the source. Reading is fundamental to me, and it has always been part of how I think and how I learn.

Outside of work, my time stays close to what matters. A lot of it is spent moving, whether that means being out on a court, on a field, or simply finding something active to do together. Lately, that has included picking up skateboarding, which started with hesitation and turned into something I genuinely enjoy. Most of this time is shared with my son, and that is what gives it weight.

Architecture and photography are not casual interests. They are things I return to because they hold my attention in a different way. Architecture is design in the real world, shaped by materials, constraints, and changing conditions that cannot be controlled. Photography builds on that through light and context. Lighting defines what is seen, but context defines what it means. It can bring a smile to someone’s face or document something that carries real consequence. It has been both for me, and that range is what keeps me connected to it. I use my camera regularly, most often the one in my pocket. It is always there, and that matters. I come from a Nikon family. My father introduced me to cameras through his Nikon FM2, and I began my journey on a D5100. I have since moved to full frame with the D800, and it has become one of my favorite creative tools. There is a different level of control there. Ultimately, it is the time to slow down, to refuel creativity, and renew intentions.

When I step away from work, I do not structure that time. Sometimes I am reading, sometimes there is a Knicks game on, and sometimes I am in the kitchen working through something simple. I have been watching the Knicks since I was barely thirteen, and I have always respected how they play. They are a gritty team, and they always have been. Grit is not a guarantee of success, but it is a fundamental part. It is what pushes me forward to shift.

taste

I do not think about tools in isolation. They show up as part of the work, and they stay only if they keep pace with how I build. The process moves back and forth between design and development, and anything that slows that down gets in the way.

Most ideas start as rough structure, something I can shape quickly before committing to it. I move between design and code without much separation, refining the feel while the implementation takes form. Xcode is where things settle once they begin to hold, and that is where the real decisions get made. When something feels off, I do not guess; I open Instruments and find out what is actually happening. Iteration stays constant, moving between the simulator and real devices until it behaves the way it should.

On the data side, the choice is usually clear. If the product stays within Apple’s ecosystem, CloudKit is where I start because it fits naturally and keeps the system aligned. When it needs to go beyond that, I reach for Supabase or Firebase depending on what the product actually needs, not what looks better on paper. The goal is to support the work without turning the backend into its own project.

There are moments where speed matters more than structure, and that is where lighter tools come in. A quick edit happens in Sublime when I need it to be fast and predictable. For design, I default to Affinity for general visual work, while Figma or Sketch come in when the focus shifts to interface and interaction. The tools adjust to the work rather than the other way around.

The setup itself does not change much. I work from a single machine, with a few devices close by, so everything stays within reach. It is less about having options and more about staying in a loop that does not break. AI fits into that loop the same way. It is useful when it helps move faster, and it is ignored when it does not. It remains part of the process, not the process itself.

Through all of it, there is always something nearby, whether it is chai, coffee, or a Vimto, depending on the day.

the standard

The standard is simple. The work has to hold up.

There is always pressure to add more, to market more, to expand beyond what the product actually needs. That pressure does not go away, so it has to be managed deliberately. The focus stays on the details, because that is where the difference is made. If something does not serve the product, it is removed, regardless of how much time was spent on it.

A minimal interface does not mean a simple system. Most of the work happens beneath the surface, where structure, behavior, and constraints are doing their job without drawing attention to themselves. That depth is easy to miss, but it is what allows everything else to feel clear.

There is always more there. It just does not need to be shown.
